1. Gwendolyn Brooks, (1917-2000)

Gwendolyn Brooksâ€™ is a familiar name in the poetry community as she is remembered for her groundbreaking publications as an African American poet. Her work spoke on the ugly truth of American topics like the poor economic conditions forced on many Black Americans and the events of the Civil Rights Movement. Not to mention, she made history as the first Black woman to serve in the role of Poetry Consultant for the Library of Congress and the first Black writer to be awarded the Pulitzer Prize.

2. Audre Lorde, (1934-1992)

Audre Lorde was a remarkable poet who spoke on the real and honest experiences she faced as a black queer woman. In addition, she spoke on the negative societal image and treatment of the African American community and her health as she battled breast cancer. Her poetry encompassed a more diverse subject matter that set the stage for other authors with similar narratives to get their voices heard as well. .

3. Robert Sengstacke Abbott, (1870-1940)

Robert Sengstacke Abbott is known in history as the founder of the Chicago Defender. Founded in 1950, it was a paper that reported on issues important to the Black community and encouraged the relocation of African Americans to the North; therefore, leaving the inhumane living conditions in the Jim Crow South. Though the Chicago Defender at humble beginnings with only four pages initially it is credited as paving the way for the various Black-focused publications we see today.
